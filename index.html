Choose Your Queen Wisely:

Royal Consort Conundrum in British History

It is a truth universally acknowledged that a person in possession of the ROYAL CROWN must be in want of a good spouse.

For most of the United Kingdom's history, Jane Austen's opening remark in Pride and Prejudice was a matter of critical national importance for a British monarch.

Choosing the right spouse was key to building or maintaining vital political alliances with Continental powers. Conversely, marrying the wrong person could weaken the legitimacy of the royal lineage and encourage dissent.


Most famously, Eleanor of Aquitaine in 1154 brought with ther a territory that was larger than the kingom of France at the time.

Catherine of Valois' marriage to Henry V in 1420 was part of a plan to eventually place his husband on the throne of France, and thereby end the Hundred Years' War.

Catherine of Braganza was picked for Charles II because of her father King John 's prominent position as the head of the powerful Portuguese colonial empire.

Alexandra of Denmark's family had been relatively obscure until 1852, when her father was chosen to become king of Denmark. She was then deemed an appropriate wife for the future Edward VII, son of Queen Victoria.
