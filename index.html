Choose Your Queen Wisely:

Royal Consort Conundrum in British History

It is a truth universally acknowledged that a person in possession of the ROYAL CROWN must be in want of a good spouse.

For most of the United Kingdom's history, Jane Austen's opening remark in Pride and Prejudice was a matter of critical national importance for a British monarch.

For this island nation, choosing the right spouse was key to building or maintaining vital political alliances with Continental powers: often, those countries were France, Spain and Germany.

Conversely, marrying the wrong person, without a proper dowry or royal connections, could weaken the legitimacy of the royal lineage and encourage dissent.

Where did these (mostly) women come from? And do their places of origin reveal historic trends? Will the data show anything unexpected?

Let's find out, using data analysis and visualization.

Note: this timeline starts with the House of Normandy (1066) and ends with the current House of Windsor, a timespan of more than 860 years. Records from the Houses of Wessex and Denmark (886-1066) are too complex and unreliable, with dates lost to time and uncrowned consorts of uncertain status. Our analysis starts with the Norman Conquest and ends with Queen Camilla.

The trends are much more visible. There are no fewer than 10 French women (blue bars) in the first 15 consorts, and 13 among the first 20. But after that number, there is a shift toward English queens (purple bars). And starting from consort #34, a series of six German spouses.

How about a cumulative view?

Stacked Bar Chart of country totals at the accession of every new consort
source: wikipedia

The stacked bar chart summarizes nicely the political and military imperatives behind the choice of women from the same country, over and over.

For most of the middle ages, from 1066 to around 1470, securing a French spouse was the best way for English sovereigns to maintain familial connections with the Kingdom of France, with which it was competing for a staggering number of territories.

Most famously, Eleanor of Aquitaine in 1154 brought with her a duchy that was larger than the kingom of France at the time.

In 1170, Margaret of France was picked because her dowry was the much disputed territory of Vexin north of Paris.

Catherine of Valois' marriage to Henry V in 1420 was part of a plan to eventually place his husband on the throne of France, and thereby end the Hundred Years' War.

Catherine of Braganza was picked for Charles II in 1660 after her father became the head of Portugal's powerful colonial empire.

Alexandra of Denmark's family had been relatively obscure until 1852, when her father became king of Denmark. She was then deemed an appropriate wife for the future Edward VII, son of Queen Victoria.

But with the end of the 19th century, the international importance of a consort diminished. Of course, royal spouses had to meet the social requirements of their time, but the monarch's choice of a consort ceased to be a matter of vital international importance.




Staying Power

We've taken a close look at the composition and sequence of British royal marriages, but could there be a more qualitative yeardstick? How about total time spent as royal consort? We've got that!


Bar chart of each consort with their time on the throne
source: wikipedia

Being a royal spouse can be a lifelong mission (as evidenced by the very pink column on the penultimate spot -- that's Prince Philip, who stayed at his post for 69 years) or evaporate in an instant: many women, even queens, died in labor, and as shown by the sobering flat entries next to the green Spanish column in the middle of the chart, made the mistake of marrying King Henry VIII to tragic results.


The Best English Queens Were... French
source: wikipedia

Given France's front-runner status in the number of British consorts it produced, its dominant position in cumulative tenure time shouldn't be a shock. Still, it is striking that England, with no fewer than 10 consorts, doesn't even reach the 100 year mark and is actually beaten by Germany.

At the same time, the low total time posted by English consorts only strengthens our thesis: that for most of history, royal marriages were above all a diplomatic tool aimed at securing the country's international standing in an ever insecure world.
